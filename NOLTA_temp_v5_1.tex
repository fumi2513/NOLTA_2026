\section{Introduction}
Deep learning has achieved remarkable success in various fields, such as image recognition and natural language processing. The generalization performance of deep neural networks has often been improved by increasing model size, the amount of training data, and computational resources\cite{kaplan_scaling_2020}. This tendency is often characterized by scaling laws, which describe how model performance improves as these resources are scaled up.

However, such resource-based scaling is not always applicable in practical situations. In some domains, such as industrial anomaly detection and medical applications, it is difficult to collect a large amount of labeled data, especially for rare or abnormal cases. In addition, some applications must be performed under limited computational resources, memory capacity, or inference time. Therefore, it is important to explore alternative learning paradigms that can improve generalization performance without simply relying on larger models, larger datasets, or larger computational budgets.

In recent years, collaborative learning frameworks using multiple models have attracted attention as one possible approach. These frameworks use the predictions of other models as additional supervision during training, thereby improving generalization performance. Since model collaboration is required only during training, these frameworks can improve model performance without increasing the computational cost at inference time.

A representative example of such frameworks is Mutual learning (ML)\cite{zhang_deep_2018}, in which multiple models are trained simultaneously while referring to each other's prediction distributions. Unlike conventional teacher-student approaches, ML does not require a fixed pre-trained teacher model. In ML, each model is trained using a loss function consisting of a standard supervised loss and a KL-divergence-based consistency loss between prediction distributions. Previous studies have shown that ML models often achieves better generalization performance than individual learning models. However, it remains unclear how ML improves model performance, particularly how interactions between models during training contribute to this improvement.

In this study, the generalization process of Mutual learning (ML) was analyzed by comparing ML models with Individual learning (IL) models. To analyze this process, we focused on the double descent phenomenon\cite{belkin_reconciling_2019,nakkiran_deep_2021}, which describes non-monotonic changes in test error as model complexity or training progress increases. We first analyzed parameter-wise double descent by varying the base number of filters and defined the under-parameterized, critically-parameterized, and over-parameterized regimes based on the resulting training and test error curves. We then analyzed the training dynamics of ML and IL across these regimes by examining how the error rates changed over training epochs.

The main contributions of this study are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
  \item We analyze ML by dividing the parameter-wise double descent behavior into three regimes and comparing its generalization behavior with that of Individual learning (IL) in each regime.

  \item We show that the effect of ML differs across regimes and is especially pronounced in the Critical Regime.
  In this regime, ML suppresses the increase in test error and achieves a lower error rate than IL.

  \item We show that ML may improve generalization by suppressing overfitting to noisy-label samples and converging to flatter minima.
  This tendency is supported by variance analysis, Hessian eigenvalue analysis, and loss landscape visualization.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Works}
\subsection{Mutual Learning}

ML is a training framework in which multiple models are trained simultaneously by referring to each other's prediction distributions.
In contrast to IL, where each model is optimized independently using only the ground-truth labels, ML introduces interactions among models through a consistency loss.

For two models, let $p_1$ and $p_2$ denote the prediction distributions of Model 1 and Model 2, respectively.
The parameters of Model 1 and Model 2 are denoted by $\Theta_1$ and $\Theta_2$, respectively.
The objective functions are defined as
\begin{equation}
L_{\Theta_1} = L_{\mathrm{sup}}(y, p_1) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_2 \| p_1),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
L_{\Theta_2} = L_{\mathrm{sup}}(y, p_2) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_1 \| p_2),
\end{equation}
where $y$ denotes the ground-truth label, and $L_{\mathrm{sup}}$ denotes the supervised loss.
The supervised loss term trains each model using the ground-truth label, while the KL divergence term acts as a consistency loss between the prediction distributions of the two models.

Here, $y$ denotes the ground-truth label, and $L_{\mathrm{sup}}$ denotes the supervised loss.
The supervised loss term trains each model using the ground-truth label, while the KL divergence term acts as a consistency loss between the prediction distributions of the two models.

Models trained with ML often improve generalization performance compared with models trained with IL.
Since each model is trained collaboratively but used independently at inference time, ML can improve the training process without increasing the inference cost of a single model.

Previous studies have suggested that posterior entropy and robust minima may be related to the improved generalization of ML~\cite{zhang_deep_2018}.
However, how the interactions introduced by ML affect the generalization process under changes in model complexity and training time has not been fully characterized.

\subsection{Double Descent}
Double descent is a phenomenon in which the test error first decreases, then increases, and finally decreases again as model complexity increases. This behavior differs from the conventional U-shaped generalization curve expected from the classical bias--variance trade-off.

\begin{figure}[htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{fig/1_double_descent.png}
  \caption{Schematic illustration of double descent and three regimes.}
  \label{fig:doubledescent}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:doubledescent} schematically illustrates this behavior. In this figure, the horizontal axis represents a complexity factor, such as the number of model parameters or the number of training epochs. When the curve is observed by increasing the number of model parameters, it is referred to as parameter-wise double descent. When it is observed as training progresses over epochs, it is referred to as epoch-wise double descent.

As indicated by the shaded regions in Fig.~\ref{fig:doubledescent}, the double descent curve is divided into three regimes: the under-parameterized regime, the critically-parameterized regime, and the over-parameterized regime, colored purple, orange, and green, respectively.
In the under-parameterized regime, the model does not have sufficient capacity to fit the training data, and increasing model complexity tends to reduce the test error. 
In the critically-parameterized regime, the model capacity is approximately matched to the complexity of the training data. In this regime, the test error tends to become sensitive to changes in complexity and often shows unstable behavior. The local maximum observed around this regime is referred to as the peak of double descent. 
In the over-parameterized regime, the model has sufficient capacity to fit the training data, and further increasing model complexity tends to reduce the test error again.

When label noise is introduced into the training labels, the peak tends to become more prominent, making double descent behavior more clearly observable.

\section{Experiments}
We first analyzed parameter-wise double descent by varying the base number of filters and defined the under-parameterized, critically-parameterized, and over-parameterized regimes based on the resulting training and test error curves. We then analyzed the training dynamics of ML and IL across these regimes by examining how the error rates changed over training epochs. To further examine the differences in generalization behavior, we conducted additional analyses based on bias--variance decomposition, the top-five Hessian eigenvalues, loss landscape geometry, and error behavior on noisy-label and clean-label training samples.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
% データセット
We evaluated ML and IL on an image classification task using CIFAR-10\cite{krizhevsky_learning_2009}.
CIFAR-10 consist of 50,000 training images and 10,000 test images from 10 classes.
We added 20\% label noise to the training labels.

% 使用モデル
For both ML and IL, we used a 5-layer CNN consisting of four convolutional layers and one fully connected layer.
The numbers of filters in the four convolutional layers were set to $[k, 2k, 4k, 8k]$, where $k$ denoted the base number of filters.

% 初期値の設定
In ML, the two models were denoted as Model 1 and Model 2. These two models were trained together from different initial weights. In IL, a single model initialized with the same initial weights as Model 1 was trained individually. We reported only the results of Model 1 for ML. We then compared them with those of the corresponding IL model. Each result was averaged over five independent runs with different initial weights.

% 損失関数＋最適化
The loss function for IL was the cross-entropy loss.
For ML, the loss function consisted of the cross-entropy loss and the KL divergence between the prediction distributions of the two models.
All models were trained for 500 epochs using SGD without momentum.
The batch size was 128, and the learning rate was set to $1/\sqrt{1+t/512}$, where $t$ denoted the training step.

\subsection{Parameter-wise Double Descent}
% Overview
This experiment compared the parameter-wise double descent behavior of ML and IL. We analyzed the resulting error curves by dividing them into regimes and comparing the behavior of ML and IL in each regime.

% Setting
To construct the parameter-wise error curves, the base number of filters $k$ was varied from 1 to 60. For each value of $k$, we trained the models for 500 epochs and used the training and test error rates at the final epoch.
% 図の説明
Figure~\ref{fig:filter_wise} shows the training and test error rates as functions of the base number of filters $k$. The blue and red curves represent IL and ML, respectively, with solid lines for the test error rates and dashed lines for the training error rates.

\begin{figure}[htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{fig/3_filter_wise.png}
  \caption{Parameter-wise double descent in IL and ML.}
  \label{fig:filter_wise}
\end{figure}

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:filter_wise}, both IL and ML exhibit parameter-wise double descent. Compared with IL, ML shows a lower test-error peak and shifts the peak toward larger values of $k$.

To further analyze this difference, we divided the parameter-wise range into three regimes: the under-parameterized, critically-parameterized, and over-parameterized regimes. 
These regimes are generally characterized by the model complexity relative to the training data. However, their exact boundaries are not strictly specified\cite{nakkiran_deep_2021}. In this study, we operationally defined the parameter-wise regimes based on the training and test error curves. The under-parameterized regime was defined as the region from the beginning of the analyzed range to the point where the test error started to increase. The critically-parameterized regime was defined as the region from the onset of the test-error increase to the point where the training error rate fell below 0.05. The over-parameterized regime was defined as the region after the training error rate fell below 0.05.

% 図の説明
Based on these definitions, the under-parameterized regime corresponds to $k=1$--$4$ for IL and $k=1$--$5$ for ML. The critically-parameterized regime corresponds to $k=5$--$11$ for IL and $k=6$--$13$ for ML, while the over-parameterized regime corresponds to $k=12$--$60$ for IL and $k=14$--$60$ for ML. Figure~\ref{fig:filterwise_regime} summarizes these regime assignments for IL and ML. 

\begin{figure}[htb]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{fig/4_filterwise_regime.png}
\caption{Regime-wise comparison of parameter-wise double descent in IL and ML.}
\label{fig:filterwise_regime}
\end{figure}

This comparison shows that the under-parameterized regime is longer in ML than in IL. The critically-parameterized regime also starts later and lasts longer in ML. The transition to the over-parameterized regime is later in ML.

\subsection{Variance Analysis Based on Bias--Variance Decomposition}
% Overview
This experiment compared the variance between ML and IL to further investigate their generalization behavior in double descent.
Variance is related to overfitting and prediction stability, and was computed using a cross-entropy-based bias--variance decomposition\cite{yang_rethinking_2020}.

For an input sample $x$, let $\pi_i(x)$ denote the prediction distribution of the $i$-th trained model.
The variance was computed by averaging the KL divergence over the trained models and the test samples:

\[
\mathrm{Variance}
=
\frac{1}{N}
\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}}|}
\sum_{x \in \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}}}
D_{\mathrm{KL}}
\left(
\bar{\pi}(x)\,\|\,\pi_i(x)
\right),
\]

where $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}}$ denotes the test set and $\bar{\pi}(x)$ is the representative prediction distribution.
Following the cross-entropy-based bias--variance decomposition, $\bar{\pi}(x)$ is defined as

\[
\bar{\pi}(x)
=
\mathrm{Normalize}
\left(
\exp\left(
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\log \pi_i(x)
\right)
\right),
\]

where $N$ is the number of trained models.
In our experiments, we set $N=5$ and used five trained models with different initial weights.
The variance was computed for each base number of filters $k$ at each training epoch.

Figure~\ref{fig:variance} shows the resulting variance heatmaps of IL and ML.
The horizontal axis represents the base number of filters $k$, the vertical axis represents the training epoch, and the color indicates the variance.
The right panel shows the difference between ML and IL.

\begin{figure}[htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{fig/9_variance.png}
  \caption{Variance heatmaps of IL and ML and their difference (ML--IL) over the base number of filters $k$ and training epochs.}
  \label{fig:variance}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:variance} shows that IL and ML have similar variance values in the under-parameterized regime. In the critically-parameterized regime, ML has lower variance than IL, indicating that ML suppresses the increase in variance observed in IL. In the over-parameterized regime, ML shows noticeable variance fluctuations during the early stage of training and often has higher variance than IL in this stage.

\subsection{Analysis of Loss Landscape Geometry}
% Overview
This experiment compared the loss landscape geometry between ML and IL.
To analyze the sharpness of the loss landscape around the obtained solutions, we first computed the top five eigenvalues of the Hessian matrix and then visualized the loss landscape around representative solutions.

\paragraph{Hessian eigenvalue analysi}
% setup
The Hessian matrix consists of the second-order derivatives of the loss function and describes the local curvature around an obtained solution. Larger Hessian eigenvalues indicate sharper local curvature. We trained the models with each base number of filters $k$ for 500 epochs, computed the five largest Hessian eigenvalues at the final epoch, and averaged them.

% 図の説明
Figure~\ref{fig:hessian} compares IL and ML in terms of the average of the five largest Hessian eigenvalues. The horizontal axis represents the base number of filters $k$, and the vertical axis represents the average of the five largest Hessian eigenvalues. The blue and red curves represent IL and ML, respectively, and the shaded regions indicate the parameter-wise regimes defined based on the parameter-wise double descent results.

Figure~\ref{fig:hessian} shows that IL and ML have comparable eigenvalues in the under-parameterized regime. \paragraph{Loss Landscape Visualization}

% setup
We visualized the loss landscape around the solutions obtained by ML and IL.
For learned model parameters $\theta^{*}$, the two-dimensional loss surface is defined as
\[
f(\alpha, \beta)
=
L(\theta^{*} + \alpha \delta + \beta \eta),
\]
where $\delta$ and $\eta$ are direction vectors, and $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are displacement coefficients.

In this experiment, we selected $k=1$, $k=10$, and $k=60$ as representative values for the under-parameterized, critically-parameterized, and over-parameterized regimes, respectively.
The displacement coefficients $\alpha$ and $\beta$ were varied within the range $[-0.2,0.2]$, forming a $51\times51$ grid.
For each of the five trained models, three sets of direction vectors were used.
The final landscape was obtained by averaging the loss changes over all models and direction sets.


% results
\begin{figure}[htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{fig/11_Loss_Landscape.png}
  \caption{Loss landscapes of IL and ML at representative values of $k$.}
  \label{fig:landscape}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:landscape} shows the loss landscapes of IL and ML for the representative values of $k$ indicated by the arrows in Fig.~\ref{fig:hessian}.
Specifically, $k=1$, $k=10$, and $k=60$ correspond to the under-parameterized, critically-parameterized, and over-parameterized regimes, respectively.

For $k=1$, both IL and ML show relatively flat landscapes, and no clear difference is observed.
For $k=10$, which corresponds to the critically-parameterized regime, the loss landscape of IL is much sharper than that of ML, whereas ML exhibits a flatter landscape.
For $k=60$, both IL and ML again show relatively flat landscapes, and the difference between the two methods becomes small.

These observations are consistent with the Hessian eigenvalue analysis.

\section{Discussion}
\paragraph{Why ML improves generalization in the Critically-Parameterized Regime.}

The experimental results indicate that Mutual learning (ML) is particularly effective in the Critically-Parameterized Regime.
In the filter-wise analysis, ML shows lower test error than Individual learning (IL) around this regime.
In addition, the peak of double descent is suppressed and shifted toward larger model widths.
These results suggest that ML changes not only the final test performance but also the process by which models fit the training data.

A possible reason for this behavior is that ML suppresses rapid overfitting to noisy labels.
In ML, each model is trained while matching its prediction distribution to that of the other model.
Therefore, unlike IL, each model does not fit noisy labels only based on its own prediction.
This interaction can delay excessive fitting to noisy-label samples and make the fitting process more gradual.
As a result, the onset of the Critically-Parameterized Regime shifts toward larger model widths.

This interpretation is also supported by the analysis of noisy-label and clean-label samples.
In the Critically-Parameterized Regime, ML converges more slowly than IL on noisy-label samples.
This suggests that ML does not simply slow down the entire training process, but rather suppresses rapid fitting to corrupted labels.
Since overfitting to noisy labels can increase the sensitivity of the model to initialization and training samples, suppressing such fitting can lead to more stable predictions.

The variance analysis further supports this interpretation.
In the Critically-Parameterized Regime, the increase in variance is suppressed by ML compared with IL.
This indicates that ML reduces the variation of prediction distributions among models trained from different initial weights.
Moreover, the Hessian eigenvalue analysis and loss landscape visualization show that ML tends to converge to flatter solutions than IL.
These results suggest that ML improves generalization by suppressing overfitting to noisy labels, reducing prediction variance, and guiding the models toward flatter minima.

\paragraph{Why ML shows different behavior across regimes.}

The effect of Mutual learning (ML) differs depending on the model-width regime.
This is because the role of model capacity and the stability of the learned solution change as the number of parameters increases.
In other words, ML does not affect all model widths in the same way, but its effect appears differently according to how easily the model can fit the training data, including noisy labels.

In the Under-Parameterized Regime, both ML and Individual learning (IL) have limited model capacity.
Therefore, neither method can fully fit the training data, and excessive fitting to noisy labels is unlikely to occur.
As a result, the solutions obtained by ML and IL tend to be similar, and the difference between their error rates remains small.
In this regime, the regularizing effect of ML is not clearly observed because the models are still constrained mainly by insufficient capacity.

In the Critically-Parameterized Regime, the model starts to fit the training data more closely.
This regime is unstable because small changes in initialization or noisy samples can strongly affect the learned solution.
Therefore, IL is more likely to overfit noisy labels and show increased test error.
In contrast, ML suppresses rapid fitting to noisy labels by aligning the prediction distributions of two models.
Thus, the effect of ML becomes most apparent in this regime, where it reduces test error, suppresses variance, and leads to flatter solutions.

In the Over-Parameterized Regime, the model has sufficiently large capacity to fit the training data.
Therefore, both ML and IL can eventually reach solutions with similar test performance.
However, the larger number of parameters also increases the freedom of possible solutions.
Since the two models in ML are initialized differently, they may follow different fitting paths in the early stage of training.
At the same time, ML forces their prediction distributions to be consistent through the KL-divergence term.
This interaction can make the learning dynamics unstable at the beginning of training, leading to larger fluctuations in the error rate.

Overall, these results suggest that the regime-dependent behavior of ML is caused by the balance between model capacity, sensitivity to noisy labels, and the freedom of possible solutions.
ML has little effect in the Under-Parameterized Regime because the models lack sufficient capacity.
It is most effective in the Critically-Parameterized Regime because the models are sensitive to noisy labels and initialization.
In the Over-Parameterized Regime, its effect appears mainly in the transient training dynamics rather than in the final test error.

\section{Conclusions}

This study analyzed the generalization process of Mutual learning (ML) through the lens of double descent.
We compared ML with Individual learning (IL) in terms of filter-wise double descent, epoch-wise double descent, variance, Hessian eigenvalues, and loss landscape geometry.

The experimental results showed that both ML and IL exhibit double descent behavior.
However, ML tended to delay the transition into the Critical Regime and suppress the increase in test error, especially around the Critical Regime.
This suggests that ML can reduce overfitting during the learning process.

The variance analysis further showed that ML suppresses the increase in variance around the Critical Regime.
In addition, the Hessian eigenvalue analysis and loss landscape visualization indicated that ML tends to converge to flatter solutions than IL.
These results suggest that the generalization improvement of ML is related not only to the final test error but also to changes in variance and loss landscape geometry.

Future work will focus on clarifying why ML achieves a lower error rate in the Critical Regime.
In particular, we plan to investigate how the similarity of learned representations between models affects this behavior, using methods such as centered kernel alignment (CKA).

\section{Guide for preparing multi-color display items}
Color figures can be included in NOLTA manuscripts. Authors, however,
should be reminded that some reviewers/readers may be colorblind.
Authors should consider the following suggestions when preparing figures:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Avoid using a combination of red and green. Use magenta (purple)
and green instead.
\item Do not overuse colors in the graphs but consider using different
hatching, shapes, line types (solid, dotted or dashed) or symbols for
different curves.
\item Red does not appear bright or vivid for those with colorblindness,
so avoid using red characters or symbols.
For further information, see
\url{http://jfly.iam.u-tokyo.ac.jp/color/index.html}.
\end{enumerate}
